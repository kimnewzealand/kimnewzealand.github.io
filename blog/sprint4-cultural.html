What is Neuroplasticity?

It appears that the brain's physical changes with its neuroplasticity in response to learning new concepts and skills over a lifetime, to continuously learn, even into adulthood.

I find that I multitask and am often distracted so sometimes feel like I am going around in circles. Focussing on my study and following firmer time boxes, not only during the EDA, but also afterwards may help increase my neuroplasticity.

There are references to scientific papers that discuss neuroplasticity on There are references to scientific papers that discuss neuroplasticity in Science Direct. The paper I found most interesting discusses 10 principals of neuroplasticity:.
  • USE IT OR LOSE IT. Failure to drive specific brain functions can lead to functional degradation.
  • USE IT AND IMPROVE IT. Training that drives brain functions can lead to enhancement of function.
  • SPECIFICITY. Nature of the training experience dictates the nature of the plasticity.
  • REPETITION MATTERS. Plasticity requires sufficient repetition.
  • INTENSITY MATTERS. Plasticity requires sufficient training intensity.
  • TIME MATTERS. Different forms of plasticity occur at different times during training and over the course of recovery.
  • TRANSFERENCE. Plasticity in response to one training experience can enhance acquisition of similar behaviors.
  • INTERFERENCE. Plasticity in response to one experience can interfere with the acquisition of behaviors.
Then one can focus on the principals to make that learning more effective.

What is the Growth Mindset?

The growth mindset is a psychological, rather than physiological concept. According to psychologist

Carol Dweck:
In a fixed mindset students believe their basic abilities, their intelligence, their talents, are just fixed traits. They have a certain amount and that’s that, and then their goal becomes to look smart all the time and never look dumb. In a growth mindset students understand that their talents and abilities can be developed through effort, good teaching and persistence. They don’t necessarily think everyone’s the same or anyone can be Einstein, but they believe everyone can get smarter if they work at it.

A growth mindset may be relevant to a combination of individual and group learning because everyone may be inclined to collectively share and learn but also enjoy the learning experience. I was initially surprised that there was discussion over some of the growth studies findings as it does sound like a compelling story. However Carol Dweck has acknowledged that:

In science, we build a firm foundation and then we keep renovating the house. We find interesting results, we are fascinated by them, we don’t always trust them, so we go back and replicate them

and

We cautioned that mindsets are not “magic bullets” but depend critically on context and delivery
Based on my reading, I would like to think more like growing and take opportunities for discussion in my learning, take on board all types of feedback, and not worry about looking 'stupid'.
